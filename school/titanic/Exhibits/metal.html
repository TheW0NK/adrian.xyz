More on the metal and hull...

Many critical design flaws were found in the titanic hull.

One of the most critical flaws was the bulkheads not extending high enough, allowing water to spill over into adjacent compartments, which was one major component contributing to the rapid sinking of the RMS Titanic.

Another critical flaw was the hull's double bottom not extending up the sides of the ship, which could have provided additional protection against breaches.

Further studies revealed that the steel used in the Titanic had a high sulfur content, which made it more brittle. This was a common issue with steel produced in the early 20th century.

Lastly, the riveting process used was not robust enough to withstand the impact with the iceberg, leading to catastrophic failure of the hull.

These design flaws, combined with the lack of lifeboats and inadequate training of the crew, led to the tragic loss of life on the Titanic.

It is important to learn from these mistakes and ensure that future ships are designed and built with safety as the top priority, which is why airplanes and cruise ships have saftey videos and drills.
